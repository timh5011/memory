# KS entropy estimation from real-world data: a cross-disciplinary survey

**Kolmogorov-Sinai entropy and its practical approximations — permutation entropy, approximate entropy, sample entropy, and Lyapunov-based estimates — have been computed from empirical data across at least eight major disciplines, with financial markets, cardiac physiology, and seismology producing the richest literatures.** The dominant finding across domains is that pathological, crisis-prone, or disturbed systems generally exhibit *reduced* complexity (lower entropy), a pattern now called the "complexity-loss hypothesis." In practice, true KS entropy is almost never computed directly from empirical data due to prohibitive data requirements; instead, three approximations dominate: sample entropy (SampEn) for biomedical signals, permutation entropy (PE) for financial and geophysical series, and Lyapunov exponent sums via Pesin's identity for physics experiments. The choice of method is dictated primarily by time series length, noise level, and whether amplitude information matters.

## Financial markets are the most prolific testing ground

The application of KS entropy concepts to financial data began in the late 1980s with the "chaos in finance" program. **Scheinkman & LeBaron (1989)** applied the Grassberger-Procaccia correlation dimension algorithm to CRSP weekly stock returns and found strong nonlinear dependence, though not definitive chaos. **Frank & Stengos (1989)** were among the first to directly estimate Kolmogorov entropy from financial data, computing **K ≈ 0.2** for daily gold and silver returns — a positive, finite value suggestive of deterministic chaos. Hsieh (1991) extended this to daily foreign exchange rates using correlation dimension and Lyapunov exponents, finding pervasive nonlinearity that GARCH models could only partially explain. The BDS test developed by **Brock, Dechert, Scheinkman & LeBaron (1996)**, built on the correlation integral, became the standard nonparametric test for nonlinear dependence in economics and has been applied to thousands of financial series.

The literature took a decisive turn toward practical entropy approximations in the 2000s. **Pincus & Kalman (2004, PNAS)** applied approximate entropy to the S&P 500 and Hang Seng indices, discovering that ApEn spiked to near-complete irregularity in the two weeks preceding the 1987 crash and the 1997 Asian crisis — establishing entropy as a potential early-warning indicator. The permutation entropy framework introduced by Bandt & Pompe (2002) spawned a parallel literature: **Zunino et al. (2009)** computed normalized permutation entropy for **32 stock exchanges worldwide**, finding that developed markets exhibit higher PE (closer to random, more efficient) while emerging markets have lower PE and more "forbidden" ordinal patterns. This established PE as a quantitative proxy for the Efficient Market Hypothesis.

More recent work has expanded the empirical base substantially. A 2022 study in *Entropy* (MDPI) computed sample entropy for **36 European and U.S. stock indices** across the Global Financial Crisis and COVID-19, finding that entropy decreased during both turbulence periods — markets became more regular and predictable during crises. Sigaki, Perc & Ribeiro (2019, *Scientific Reports*) applied PE to **437 cryptocurrencies**, finding Bitcoin's efficiency at approximately 0.85 (fraction of time within random confidence bands). Bariviera (2017) and Martina et al. (2011) applied multiscale sample entropy to crude oil prices, discovering a **~4.3-year periodicity** in entropy that mirrors Kitchin business cycles, with U.S. recessions coinciding with reduced entropy. Sandubete & Escot (2020) used neural-network Lyapunov exponent estimation on **tick-by-tick forex data** from 14 major currency pairs, detecting chaotic signals that disappear at daily frequency — suggesting that temporal aggregation destroys the chaotic signature.

The consensus after three decades is nuanced: strong nonlinear dependence exists in financial markets, but evidence for low-dimensional deterministic chaos remains weak at daily frequencies. The dominant pattern is that **entropy decreases during crises** (increased regularity, greater predictability), though at least one study of NYSE multifractal dynamics (2023) found the opposite at certain scales. Higher entropy generally correlates with greater market efficiency, and entropy dynamics support the Adaptive Market Hypothesis over the static EMH.

## Sociology, political science, and social systems offer growing applications

Entropy-based analysis of social and political systems traces to Henri Theil's foundational work. **Theil (1967, 1969)** introduced the Theil index — derived directly from Shannon entropy — as a decomposable measure of income inequality and applied Shannon entropy to Dutch parliamentary election data to evaluate proportional representation. The Theil index remains one of the two standard inequality measures used by the World Bank and OECD, valued for its unique property of additive decomposability into between-group and within-group components that the Gini coefficient lacks.

Electoral entropy has become a small but active subfield. **Gill (2005, *Electoral Studies*)** computed Shannon entropy from 1994 American National Election Study data, finding that entropy-based voter uncertainty about candidates' positions significantly affected spatial voting models — the "Contract with America" measurably reduced Republican candidate position entropy. **Marmani et al. (2020, *Entropy*)** analyzed complete Italian election data at the municipality level from **1948 to 2018** using b-ary entropy and Tsallis entropy, finding that contemporaneous ballots at different government levels show measurable coherence and divergence in entropy distributions. Ferreira & Dionísio (2012) computed Shannon entropy of vote share distributions across **15 European countries**, finding higher entropy correlated with political fragmentation and population dissatisfaction.

Transfer entropy has emerged as the leading tool for measuring directed causal influence in social systems. **Lee & Daniels (2023, *PNAS*)** applied transfer entropy to Armed Conflict Location & Event Data (ACLED) to construct causal networks of conflict propagation, discovering a mesoscale (weeks to months, tens to hundreds of kilometers) where long-range correlations in conflict dynamics emerge. A 2025 study in *Royal Society Open Science* computed sample entropy and refined composite multiscale entropy from **Google Trends psycholinguistic data** (first-person plural pronoun usage in Ukrainian and Russian, 2003–2025) as a proxy for social cohesion during the Russia-Ukraine conflict, detecting distinct entropy signatures for different phases of interstate aggression.

For legislative behavior, **Lee et al. (2025, arXiv)** applied a restricted Boltzmann machine framework to **U.S. Senate roll-call voting records from the 97th through 117th Congresses** (1981–2022), computing voter entropy that revealed downward trends in recent Congresses with distributions increasingly separating by party. A study using V-Dem data for **172 countries from 1900 to 2021** applied diffusion maps from statistical physics to regime dynamics, finding that democracies behave as sub-diffusive (slow, stable) systems while autocracies are super-diffusive (rapid, volatile) — with middle-range regimes showing the highest conflict propensity.

Urban segregation research relies heavily on the **Theil H-index** (entropy-based information theory index), which quantifies how diversity in each areal unit differs from city-wide diversity. **Iyer et al. (2023)** applied network Shannon entropy to LEHD Origin-Destination Employment Statistics for **25 U.S. cities**, capturing experiential segregation through commuting patterns that residential-only measures miss. Human mobility entropy studies using mobile phone data from all U.S. states have found correlations between mobility regularity (low entropy) and macroeconomic indicators including GDP and employment.

## Neuroscience, seismology, and physiology yield the strongest predictive results

The biomedical domain produced the foundational entropy approximation methods and remains their most successful application area. **Pincus (1991, PNAS)** introduced approximate entropy specifically for short cardiovascular time series, demonstrating classification of healthy versus diseased cardiac states from as few as 1,000 data points. **Costa, Goldberger & Peng (2002, 2005)** developed multiscale entropy and applied it to **PhysioNet 24-hour Holter recordings** from healthy young subjects, healthy elderly, atrial fibrillation patients, and congestive heart failure patients, finding that each condition produces a distinct MSE curve profile — lower complexity consistently indicates disease. This "complexity-loss hypothesis" has been validated across aging, cardiac disease, and neonatal risk.

Seizure prediction represents perhaps the most dramatic success. **Yang et al. (2018, *Frontiers in Computational Neuroscience*)** applied permutation entropy to intracranial EEG from **19 epilepsy patients** (University Hospital of Freiburg database), achieving **94% sensitivity with an average 62-minute prediction horizon** before seizures. Ra, Li & Li (2021) used PE-based channel selection on the **CHB-MIT scalp EEG database** (22 patients), improving prediction accuracy by over 10 percentage points compared to using all channels.

Seismology has produced equally compelling results. **Posadas et al. (2023, *Natural Hazards and Earth System Sciences*)** computed Shannon entropy over **8 years of IPOC seismic catalog data (>100,000 earthquakes in northern Chile)**, detecting entropy signatures associated with the 2007 Mw 7.8 Tocopilla and 2014 Mw 8.1 Iquique earthquakes. **Sudibyo et al. (2022)** applied permutation entropy to continuous seismic data from Shinmoedake volcano during three eruption cycles, finding that **PE decreased before each eruption** due to deterministic volcanic tremor. Vogel et al. (2023) found that Tsallis entropy increases and mutability decreases approximately **2 years before major earthquakes**, suggesting potential long-range forecasting capability.

Hydrology and climate applications round out the natural sciences. **Stosic et al. (2016)** applied permutation entropy to daily streamflow of Brazil's São Francisco River before and after dam construction, detecting how anthropogenic intervention fundamentally altered flow complexity. Serinaldi et al. (2014) used the complexity-entropy causality plane on **80 daily streamflow series from the continental U.S. over 75 years**. In climate science, Mihailović et al. (2013) found that Kolmogorov complexity and sample entropy of UV-B radiation dose time series from Serbia decreased in the post-conflict period (1999–2007), attributed to increased air pollution.

Sports science has adopted entropy measures for performance assessment. Schütte et al. (2017) found correlations of **r = 0.76** between sample entropy of vertical trunk acceleration and VO₂ in adolescent endurance athletes, enabling non-invasive estimation of aerobic capacity. Goncalves et al. (2019) used multiscale entropy to differentiate running environments and their effects on physiological regulation across 12 amateur runners in three conditions.

## Five methods dominate, each suited to different data conditions

The methodological landscape has consolidated around five primary approaches, each with distinct tradeoffs. The **Grassberger-Procaccia algorithm** estimates the correlation entropy K₂ (a lower bound on KS entropy) from the scaling behavior of the correlation integral. It requires the longest time series — the Eckmann-Ruelle condition limits reliable dimension estimation to **D ≤ 2 log₁₀ N**, meaning 1,000 data points can only reliably detect dimensions up to ~6 — and is highly sensitive to noise. This method dominated the 1980s–1990s chaos-detection literature but has largely been supplanted for empirical work.

**Approximate entropy (ApEn)**, introduced by Pincus in 1991, was the first method designed explicitly for short, noisy empirical data. It works with as few as 200–300 data points (m=2, r=0.2×SD). However, it suffers from a self-matching bias that makes signals appear more regular than they are, and its values depend on time series length. **Sample entropy (SampEn)**, developed by Richman & Moorman (2000), eliminates self-matches and produces more consistent results. It is mathematically identical to the Grassberger-Procaccia K₂ except that the limiting procedures needed for invariance are not taken. SampEn has become the standard for biomedical time series analysis, particularly heart rate variability, EMG, and EEG.

**Permutation entropy (PE)**, introduced by Bandt & Pompe (2002), operates on ordinal rank patterns rather than amplitude values. Its key advantages are computational speed (O(N) versus O(N²) for SampEn), excellent noise robustness, invariance under nonlinear monotonic transformations, and requiring only one free parameter beyond embedding dimension (the delay τ, with no tolerance r to tune). PE converges to KS entropy as embedding dimension increases, though this convergence requires very long series. It has become the method of choice for financial efficiency studies and geophysical applications. Its main limitation is discarding amplitude information, addressed by weighted and amplitude-aware variants.

**Lyapunov exponent estimation** via Pesin's identity (h_KS = Σλᵢ for λᵢ > 0) provides an indirect route to KS entropy. Methods include the Rosenstein algorithm, Wolf algorithm, and neural-network approaches (Nychka-Ellner-Gallant-McCaffrey). This approach was experimentally confirmed by Hart et al. (2015, *PNAS*), who showed that in a chaotic optoelectronic oscillator, the entropy rate equals the metric entropy from positive Lyapunov exponents. **Recurrence quantification analysis** offers yet another route, estimating entropy from the distribution of diagonal line lengths in recurrence plots, with the advantage of being applicable to non-stationary data.

## Data requirements and common pitfalls shape what is feasible

Several practical constraints determine which systems are amenable to KS entropy analysis. The most fundamental is **time series length**: the Grassberger-Procaccia algorithm requires roughly 10^D data points for dimension D, making it impractical for dimensions above 5–6 even with modern datasets. ApEn and SampEn need a minimum of ~200 points (preferably 1,000+), while PE requires N >> n! (where n is the embedding dimension). Financial daily data for major indices typically provides 5,000–15,000 points over 20–60 years; high-frequency intraday data can provide millions of points but introduces non-stationarity. Physiological recordings (ECG, EEG at 256–10,000 Hz) routinely provide sufficient length.

**Stationarity** is a critical assumption that empirical data routinely violates. Non-stationarity can produce spurious identification of chaos — a key reason the "chaos in economics" debate remained unresolved for decades. Standard practice requires stationarity testing (recurrence plots, formal tests), windowing for time-varying analysis, and surrogate data testing to verify that detected nonlinear structure exceeds what linear stochastic processes can explain. The surrogate data method of **Theiler et al. (1992)** — generating phase-randomized surrogates that preserve the power spectrum — remains the essential validation step before any entropy claim.

**Sampling frequency** presents a Goldilocks problem. Undersampling destroys temporal dependence and chaotic signatures (Sandubete & Escot showed chaos in tick-by-tick forex data that vanishes at daily frequency). Oversampling creates artificial temporal correlations that inflate apparent regularity. The embedding delay τ should be chosen via the first minimum of mutual information (Fraser & Swinney) or the first zero of the autocorrelation function. A Theiler window excluding temporally proximate pairs is essential for correlation integral methods.

**Preprocessing** typically involves normalization (setting tolerance r as a fraction of standard deviation), detrending (linear or via empirical mode decomposition), and choosing embedding parameters via false nearest neighbors for dimension and mutual information for delay. The product mτ — the time span of each embedding vector — is often more important than either parameter alone.

- **Best-suited datasets**: Long (N > 1,000), approximately stationary, regularly sampled, with moderate noise — exemplified by 24-hour ECG recordings, daily stock index returns over decades, multi-year seismic catalogs, and continuous river flow gauging
- **Challenging datasets**: Short macroeconomic series (quarterly GDP provides ~200 points over 50 years), highly non-stationary series, and intermittent or event-based data
- **Standard validation**: Surrogate data testing, sensitivity analysis over parameters (r, m, τ), comparison across multiple entropy methods, and checking the Eckmann-Ruelle bound

## What entropy reveals follows a remarkably consistent pattern

Across disciplines, several cross-cutting findings emerge. First, the **complexity-loss hypothesis** — that disturbed, diseased, or crisis-prone systems exhibit reduced entropy — holds remarkably broadly. Cardiac disease reduces heart rate entropy. Financial crises reduce market entropy (with occasional scale-dependent exceptions). Volcanic eruptions are preceded by PE decreases. Dam construction reduces streamflow entropy. Aging reduces neuromuscular entropy. This suggests a universal principle: healthy, well-functioning complex systems operate near maximum complexity, and departures toward regularity signal dysfunction or impending critical transitions.

Second, **entropy serves as an effective proxy for efficiency** in financial markets: higher PE consistently correlates with market efficiency across 30+ country indices, hundreds of cryptocurrencies, and multiple commodity classes. Third, entropy dynamics are **scale-dependent** — multiscale entropy analysis frequently reveals different patterns at daily versus weekly versus monthly timescales, with short-scale entropy often behaving differently from long-scale entropy during the same events.

The field has progressively moved from asking "is this system chaotic?" (1980s–1990s) toward using entropy as a **practical diagnostic and predictive tool** (2000s–present). The shift from Grassberger-Procaccia to SampEn and PE reflects a pragmatic recognition that the theoretical ideal of computing true KS entropy matters less than having robust, interpretable complexity measures that work with real-world data limitations. The most impactful applications — seizure prediction at 94% sensitivity, earthquake precursor detection with 2-year lead times, and financial crisis early warning — demonstrate that even approximate entropy measures carry substantial practical value when applied to sufficiently rich empirical datasets.
